Complete ruleset for the game system

TIER 0 WEAPONS & MODULES

Tier 0 represents baseline engineering: simple platforms enhanced by a single, defining module.

At this tier, weapons do not win by raw damage. They win by control, reliability, or escalation discipline.

At character creation, each player chooses:

  1. One Base Weapon Platform
  2. One Tier 0 Module

No stacking. No prerequisites. One rule change per weapon.

CORE COMBAT ASSUMPTIONS

  • Base Damage: 1d6
  • Base Difficulty: 10
  • Base Instability: 0
  • Damage is converted to Severity, not subtracted from HP

BASE WEAPON PLATFORMS

Platforms define range, engagement rules, and inherent advantages.
They do not grant traits.

RANGED PLATFORM

Range: Medium

Engagement Penalty:

  • Attacks against Engaged targets: +2 Difficulty
  • Cannot Critically Succeed against Engaged targets

Mobility Constraint:

  • Move + Attack in the same turn: +1 Difficulty

Role: Safe pressure, positioning, sustained fire

MELEE PLATFORM

Range: Engaged

Close Control (Passive):

On a Clean Success, choose one:

  • Reduce target Instability by 1
  • Increase target Instability by 1

Opportunity Pressure:

If a target disengages without spending an action, you may make a free attack at +2 Difficulty

Role: Control, disruption, decisive engagement

PLAYER STATS & USING WEAPONS

This section explains how your character's abilities interact with weapons and devices.

In Axiom & Crucible, weapons are machines. They do not become stronger because you are heroic. They become safer, more precise, and more controllable in skilled hands.

THE FIVE CORE STATS

Your character has five core stats. Each answers a practical question.

Cognition — "Do you understand the system?"

Domain: Knowledge & prediction
Failure Type: Hidden faults get worse

  • Identifies problems early
  • Predicts cascading failures
  • Reduces unknown risks
Cognition acts before failure.

Precision — "Can you control it exactly?"

Domain: Accuracy & fine control
Failure Type: Misalignment, stray force

  • Affects targeting and delicate operation
  • Determines where output goes
Precision affects intent vs execution.

Force — "How much output can you physically channel?"

Domain: Load tolerance
Failure Type: Recoil, backlash, overstrain

  • Sets safe Instability threshold
  • Governs overclock tolerance
  • Never mitigates consequences after failure
Force governs how far you can push.

Resilience — "How badly does failure hurt you?"

Domain: Damage conversion
Failure Type: Injury escalation

  • Converts Injury → Strain
  • Reduces escalation severity
  • Never prevents failure
Resilience governs damage severity, not survival chance.

Adaptation — "Can you recover in the moment?"

Domain: Real-time response
Failure Type: Failure persistence

  • Clears jams
  • Stabilizes systems mid-failure
  • Reduces duration of bad states
Adaptation governs recovery, not prevention.

STARTING STATS

Standard Array: 2, 1, 1, 0, -1.

USING WEAPONS

When you use a weapon, you roll:

1d20 + relevant stat

The stat depends on what you are doing:

  • Aiming → Precision
  • Powering or bracing → Force
  • Diagnosing behavior → Cognition
  • Recovering from failure → Adaptation

You do not add damage from your stats.

COMBAT FLOW (OPERATOR SEQUENCE)

This section describes the exact order of operations when using weapons in combat. Follow it top to bottom. If a step is skipped, the machine behaves unpredictably.

STEP 1: DECLARE INTENT

State what you are trying to do, not the numbers.

Examples:

  • "I fire a controlled shot."
  • "I suppress the area."
  • "I overload the emitter and punch through the armor."

Intent determines which stat applies and which traits are relevant.

STEP 2: SET BASE PROFILE

Write down the weapon's base values:

  • Difficulty
  • Damage
  • Current Instability

No modifiers yet.

STEP 3: DECLARE OVERCLOCKING

Choose how many times you overclock before rolling.

For each overclock:

  • +2 Difficulty
  • +1 Instability
  • +1d4 Damage

Mark Instability immediately. Once declared, overclocks cannot be reduced.

STEP 4: APPLY TRAITS & MODULES

Apply weapon traits in this strict order:

  1. Die Shape Traits (Stable, Chaotic, etc.)
  2. Failure-Bias Traits (Forgiving, Brittle, etc.)
  3. Control Traits (Tunable, Buffered, etc.)

If multiple traits conflict, the weapon behaves unpredictably (GM decides the worse interpretation).

STEP 5: CHECK FORCE VS INSTABILITY

Compare current Instability to your Force:

  • Instability ≤ Force → Weapon is strained
  • Instability > Force → Weapon is unstable
  • Instability ≥ Force + 3 → Weapon is critical

This does not stop the roll. It determines how failure is interpreted.

STEP 6: ROLL

Roll: d20 (or replaced die) + relevant stat

Compare the result to the final Difficulty.

RESULT BANDS & MARGINS

Margin Outcome
Success by 5+ Critical Success
Success Clean Success
Fail by 1–4 Partial Failure
Fail by 5+ Hard Failure

OVERCLOCKING & MALFUNCTIONS

In Axiom & Crucible, weapons are machines operating near their tolerance limits.
You may push them harder — but machines always push back.

OVERCLOCKING (VOLUNTARY)

What Is Overclocking?

Overclocking is the act of deliberately driving a weapon beyond its safe operating parameters to increase output.

Overclocking is declared before rolling.

Base Weapon Profile

Every weapon has:

  • Difficulty (Target Number)
  • Damage
  • Instability (Passive instability gain per use, generally 0)
  • Instability Gauge (max instability)

Example weapon:

  • Difficulty: 10
  • Damage: 2d6
  • Instability: 0
  • Instability Gauge: 3

Multiple Overclocks

You may overclock multiple times in a single action. Each overclock stacks fully.

Example (3 Overclocks):

  • Difficulty: 16
  • Damage: 2d6 + 3d4
  • Instability: 3

There is no hard limit on overclocking — only consequences.

MALFUNCTION STATES

When you fail a weapon roll, check the weapon's current Instability:

Instability ≤ Instability gauge

Minor fault - Reduced damage, momentary loss of control, or warning signs

Instability > Instability gauge

Major malfunction - Jam, feedback injury, component damage, or uncontrolled discharge

Instability ≥ Double the Instability gauge

Catastrophic failure - Structural damage, explosions, or cascading system collapse

HEALTH, INJURY, AND TRAUMA

In Axiom & Crucible, health is not a number you lose.
It is a record of what has gone wrong.

Characters are not worn down by combat — they are compromised by failure.

THE THREE CONDITION TRACKS

Every character has three condition tracks:

1. Strain – Short-term overload

Maximum Strain: 6 + Resilience

You gain Strain when:

  • You suffer recoil or backlash
  • A weapon malfunctions at low Instability
  • You are hit by glancing or controlled attacks
  • You overclock and narrowly fail

Recovery: Clears at the end of a scene, after rest, or via medical attention

2. Injury – Lasting physical harm

Maximum Injuries: 2 + ⌊Resilience / 2⌋

You gain Injury when:

  • Strain overflows
  • You suffer a major malfunction
  • You take a heavy or uncontrolled hit
  • Instability exceeds Force during failure

Recovery: Requires time, medical care, repair, surgery, or augmentation

3. Trauma – Permanent or life-altering damage

Trauma Capacity: 2 slots by default

You gain Trauma when:

  • You suffer a catastrophic malfunction
  • You would gain Injury while at maximum Injuries
  • You are exposed to extreme feedback or explosion

Death: If you would gain Trauma and all Trauma slots are filled, the character dies.

Damage Total Severity Condition Gained
1–3 Negligible No condition (narrative only)
